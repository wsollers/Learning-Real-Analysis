\vspace{0.75em}

\noindent\textbf{Goal.}
Show that negation on $\mathbb{Z}$ is well-defined:
if $(a {-\!\!-} b) = (a' {-\!\!-} b')$, then
$-(a {-\!\!-} b) = -(a' {-\!\!-} b')$.

\vspace{0.75em}

\noindent\textbf{Logical form.}
$\forall a,b,a',b' \in \mathbb{N}:\;
a + b' = a' + b \;\Rightarrow\; b + a' = b' + a$.

\vspace{0.75em}

\noindent\textbf{Available toolkit.}
Definition~4.1.1 (equality of integers),
Definition~4.1.4 (negation: $-(a{-\!\!-}b) := b{-\!\!-}a$),
commutativity of addition in $\mathbb{N}$ (P2.2.4).
