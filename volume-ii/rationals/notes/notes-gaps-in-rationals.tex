\subsection{Gaps in the Rational Numbers}

% ---------------------------------------------------------
% Teacher exposition
% ---------------------------------------------------------

\begin{remark}[Teacher exposition]
\label{rem:q-gaps-teacher}

Although the rational numbers are dense in the real line, they do not
form a complete number system.  Certain points on the number line
cannot be represented by rational numbers.

One of the most famous examples is the number $\sqrt{2}$.

There exist rational numbers arbitrarily close to $\sqrt{2}$, but no
rational number equals it.  Thus the rational numbers approximate
$\sqrt{2}$ but never actually reach it.

This phenomenon reveals that the rational number system contains
``gaps''.

\end{remark}

% ---------------------------------------------------------
% Theorem
% ---------------------------------------------------------

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:sqrt2-irrational}

The number $\sqrt{2}$ is not rational.

\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}

Suppose, for contradiction, that

\[
\sqrt{2} = \frac{p}{q}
\]

for integers $p,q$ with $\gcd(p,q)=1$.

Squaring both sides gives

\[
2 = \frac{p^2}{q^2}.
\]

Thus

\[
p^2 = 2q^2.
\]

Hence $p^2$ is even, which implies $p$ is even.  Write

\[
p = 2k.
\]

Substituting gives

\[
(2k)^2 = 2q^2
\]

so

\[
4k^2 = 2q^2.
\]

Dividing by $2$ yields

\[
2k^2 = q^2.
\]

Thus $q^2$ is even, which implies $q$ is even.

But then both $p$ and $q$ are even, contradicting the assumption that
$\gcd(p,q)=1$.

Therefore $\sqrt{2}$ is not rational.

\end{proof}

% ---------------------------------------------------------
% Gap interpretation
% ---------------------------------------------------------

\begin{remark}[The gap around $\sqrt{2}$]
\label{rem:sqrt2-gap}

Consider the sets

\[
A = \{r \in \mathbb{Q} : r^2 < 2\}
\]

and

\[
B = \{r \in \mathbb{Q} : r^2 > 2\}.
\]

Every rational number lies in one of these sets.

Numbers in $A$ are rational approximations to $\sqrt{2}$ from below,
while numbers in $B$ approximate $\sqrt{2}$ from above.

However, there is no rational number whose square is exactly $2$.

Thus the rational number system contains a gap at $\sqrt{2}$.

\end{remark}

% ---------------------------------------------------------
% Approximation remark
% ---------------------------------------------------------

\begin{remark}[Approximating $\sqrt{2}$]

Although $\sqrt{2}$ is not rational, rational numbers can approximate
it arbitrarily closely.

For example,

\[
1.4,\quad 1.41,\quad 1.414,\quad 1.4142
\]

are rational numbers that increasingly approximate $\sqrt{2}$.

These approximations form a Cauchy sequence of rational numbers.

\end{remark}

% ---------------------------------------------------------
% Conceptual significance
% ---------------------------------------------------------

\begin{remark}[Conceptual significance]

The existence of such gaps shows that the rational numbers are
\emph{incomplete}.  Some sequences of rational numbers converge toward
limits that are not themselves rational numbers.

To remove these gaps, mathematicians extend the rational numbers to
the real numbers.

One way to do this is to define real numbers as equivalence classes of
Cauchy sequences of rational numbers.

\end{remark}
