\caption{The geometry of rational approximation. 
At the top, neighboring fractions in a Farey sequence are separated by the exact gap
$\frac{1}{bd}$, and the mediant splits this interval into two smaller gaps.
In the middle, repeated mediant insertion generates the Stern--Brocot tree.
At the bottom, rational numbers with increasing denominators approximate a real number
$x$ with rapidly shrinking error, reflecting Dirichlet-type bounds.}
\label{fig:geometry-rational-approximation}

\begin{remark}
This figure summarizes several of the central geometric ideas governing
the rational numbers.

At the top, Farey neighbors
\[
\frac{a}{b} < \frac{c}{d}
\]
are separated by the exact distance
\[
\frac{c}{d}-\frac{a}{b}=\frac{1}{bd}.
\]
The mediant
\[
\frac{a+c}{b+d}
\]
lies between them and splits the original interval into two smaller
intervals:
\[
\frac{a+c}{b+d}-\frac{a}{b}=\frac{1}{b(b+d)},
\qquad
\frac{c}{d}-\frac{a+c}{b+d}=\frac{1}{d(b+d)}.
\]

In the middle, repeated mediant insertion generates the Stern--Brocot
tree, which contains every positive rational number exactly once.

At the bottom, one sees the approximation viewpoint: rational numbers
with growing denominators lie closer and closer to a real number $x$.
Dirichlet's theorem shows that these approximations can often be made
surprisingly sharp, with error on the order of $\frac{1}{q^2}$.

Thus Farey sequences, mediants, Stern--Brocot refinement, and Dirichlet
approximation are not isolated topics. They are different manifestations
of the same hidden geometry of the rational numbers.
\end{remark}
