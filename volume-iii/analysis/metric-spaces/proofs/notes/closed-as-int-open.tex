\begin{proof}
\Claim Let $(X,d)$ be a metric space, $x \in X$, $r > 0$.
Then $\overline{B}_r(x) = \bigcap_{\varepsilon > 0} B_{r+\varepsilon}(x)$.

\Given A metric space $(X,d)$, a centre $x \in X$, and radius $r > 0$.

\Goal To show the two-way set containment.

\Strategy
$(\subseteq)$: If $d(x,y) \le r$, then $d(x,y) \le r < r + \varepsilon$
for every $\varepsilon > 0$, so $y$ is in every $B_{r+\varepsilon}(x)$.

$(\supseteq)$: If $y$ is in every $B_{r+\varepsilon}(x)$, then
$d(x,y) < r + \varepsilon$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$.
What does that force about $d(x,y)$ relative to $r$?

\begin{remark*}[Proof shape]
The interesting direction is $(\supseteq)$: ``less than $r+\varepsilon$ for
all $\varepsilon > 0$'' forces $\le r$ by the infimum characterisation.
\end{remark*}

\begin{remark*}[Dependencies]
\begin{itemize}
  \item Definition of open ball $B_r(x)$: $d(x,y) < r$.
  \item Definition of closed ball $\overline{B}_r(x)$: $d(x,y) \le r$.
  \item Real analysis: $\forall\,\varepsilon > 0,\; a < b + \varepsilon \Rightarrow a \le b$.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark*}
