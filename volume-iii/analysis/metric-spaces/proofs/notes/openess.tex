\subsection*{Openness vs Completeness: A Conceptual Distinction}
\label{sec:openness-vs-completeness-note}

\begin{remark*}[These are very different kinds of properties]
Both openness and completeness appear early in metric space theory,
and it is easy to conflate them. They describe entirely different aspects
of a space.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Openness is a topological (local) property.}
A set $U \subseteq X$ is open if every point of $U$ has a ball around it
still inside $U$. Openness depends only on the topology (the system of
open sets), not on the specific distances.
Two equivalent metrics on the same space define the same open sets.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Completeness is a metric (global) property.}
A metric space $(X,d)$ is complete if every Cauchy sequence converges
in $X$. Completeness depends on the specific metric $d$:
two equivalent metrics on the same space can disagree on completeness
(see Section~\ref{sec:completeness-not-topological}).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Open sets do not help you decide if a Cauchy sequence converges.}
The sequence $1, 1/2, 1/3, \ldots$ in $(0,1)$ is Cauchy, and
whether it converges depends on whether the limit $0$ is in the space ---
a question about completeness. The open sets of $(0,1)$ say nothing about this.
\end{remark*}

\begin{remark*}[The interplay: closed subsets of complete spaces]
The connection between the two notions is:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A closed subspace of a complete space is complete
        (Proposition~\ref{prop:closed-subspace-complete}).
  \item A complete subspace of any metric space is closed
        (Proposition~\ref{prop:complete-subspace-closed}).
\end{enumerate}

So in a complete ambient space, \emph{closed} and \emph{complete} are equivalent
properties for subspaces. But in an incomplete space like $\mathbb{Q}$,
a subset can be closed (in $\mathbb{Q}$) without being complete.
\end{remark*}
