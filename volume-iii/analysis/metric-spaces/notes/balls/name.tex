\begin{remark*}[Translating English statements about sequences into inequalities]

Suppose a problem states:

\begin{quote}
``The maximum distance between any of the three sequences
$x_n, y_n, z_n$ is $A$.'' 
\end{quote}

The key task is to translate this English description of motion into a
precise mathematical inequality.

\medskip

\textbf{Step 1: Identify the type of statement.}

This is \emph{not} a convergence statement.  
It does not compare a moving sequence to a fixed target such as $L$.

Instead, the statement concerns the \emph{relative motion of several
moving objects}.  Thus it is a \emph{bounding problem}, specifically a
\emph{dynamical bounding problem}: as the sequences move, how far apart
are they allowed to get from one another?

\medskip

\textbf{Step 2: Identify the objects.}

The objects are the sequences
\[
x_n, \quad y_n, \quad z_n .
\]

For each fixed index $n$, these correspond to three points on the real
line.  Thus at time $n$ we may imagine three moving points
\[
x_n, \; y_n, \; z_n .
\]

\medskip

\textbf{Step 3: Interpret what ``distance between sequences'' means.}

A sequence is an infinite object, but the statement is really about the
distances between their \emph{values at the same index}.  

For each $n$, we therefore examine the pairwise distances
\[
|x_n-y_n|, \qquad |x_n-z_n|, \qquad |y_n-z_n|.
\]

This is the crucial modeling step:

\begin{quote}
``distance between the sequences''
$\;\Rightarrow\;$
``distance between their values at the same index.''
\end{quote}

\medskip

\textbf{Step 4: Interpret ``between any two.''}

The phrase ``between any of the three sequences'' means that the
distance must be controlled for every pair.

With three objects there are exactly three pairs:
\[
(x_n,y_n), \qquad (x_n,z_n), \qquad (y_n,z_n).
\]

Thus the English statement is asserting that every pairwise distance
is bounded by $A$:
\[
|x_n-y_n| \le A, \qquad
|x_n-z_n| \le A, \qquad
|y_n-z_n| \le A .
\]

\medskip

\textbf{Step 5: Interpret ``maximum distance.''}

Another way to express this condition is to say that the largest of the
three pairwise distances does not exceed $A$:
\[
\max\{\,|x_n-y_n|,\ |x_n-z_n|,\ |y_n-z_n|\,\} \le A
\qquad \text{for all } n .
\]

These two formulations are equivalent.

\medskip

\textbf{Geometric interpretation.}

At each index $n$, the three points $x_n, y_n, z_n$ lie within a cluster
whose diameter is at most $A$.  In other words, no two of the three
points are ever separated by more than $A$.

On the real line this can also be expressed as
\[
\max\{x_n,y_n,z_n\} - \min\{x_n,y_n,z_n\} \le A ,
\]
since the largest distance between any two real numbers in the set is
the distance between the smallest and the largest.

\end{remark*}




\subsection{Mastering the Triangle Inequality}
\label{sec:triangle-inequality-toolkit}

\paragraph{Distance Management in Analysis.}

To master analysis, one must stop viewing the triangle inequality as
merely a geometric fact about triangles and begin to see it as a tool
for \emph{managing distances}.

In almost every proof in analysis one attempts to control a quantity of
the form
\[
|A - B|,
\]
which represents the distance between two numbers on the real line.

Often this distance cannot be measured directly. Instead, we construct
a path through intermediate points whose distances we can measure.

The triangle inequality states
\[
|x + y| \le |x| + |y|,
\]
or equivalently
\[
|A - C| \le |A - B| + |B - C|.
\]

This expresses the simple geometric idea that

\begin{quote}
The direct distance between two points is never larger than the
distance obtained by traveling through an intermediate point.
\end{quote}

Thus in proofs the triangle inequality provides a method for breaking
complicated distances into smaller, manageable pieces.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{1. The Bridge Technique (Addition and Subtraction)}

\paragraph{Logical situation.}

Suppose two sequences converge:
\[
x_n \to L,
\qquad
y_n \to M.
\]

We want to prove
\[
x_n + y_n \to L + M.
\]

The quantity we must control is
\[
\bigl|(x_n + y_n) - (L + M)\bigr|.
\]

At first glance this expression appears difficult because both $x_n$
and $y_n$ are moving.

However, the total error comes from two independent sources:

\begin{itemize}
\item the error in approximating $L$ by $x_n$,
\item the error in approximating $M$ by $y_n$.
\end{itemize}

The triangle inequality allows us to separate these errors.

\paragraph{Algebraic manipulation.}

Rewrite
\[
(x_n + y_n) - (L + M)
=
(x_n - L) + (y_n - M).
\]

Applying the triangle inequality gives
\[
|(x_n - L) + (y_n - M)|
\le
|x_n - L| + |y_n - M|.
\]

Thus the total error is bounded by the sum of the individual errors.

If we ensure
\[
|x_n - L| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2},
\qquad
|y_n - M| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2},
\]
then
\[
|(x_n + y_n) - (L + M)|
<
\frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2}
=
\varepsilon.
\]

This technique appears constantly in proofs involving sums of limits.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{2. The Cross-Term Technique (Products)}

\paragraph{Logical situation.}

Suppose we want to control
\[
|x_n y_n - LM|.
\]

This represents the difference between two rectangle areas.

The difficulty is that both sides change simultaneously, so the terms
are entangled.

The idea is to introduce an intermediate term that allows us to change
one factor at a time.

We imagine moving from
\[
x_n y_n
\quad\text{to}\quad
LM
\]
in two steps:

\begin{enumerate}
\item change $y_n$ to $M$,
\item change $x_n$ to $L$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Algebraic manipulation.}

Insert the intermediate term $x_n M$:
\[
|x_n y_n - LM|
=
|x_n y_n - x_n M + x_n M - LM|.
\]

Grouping terms yields
\[
=
|x_n(y_n - M) + M(x_n - L)|.
\]

Applying the triangle inequality gives
\[
|x_n(y_n - M) + M(x_n - L)|
\le
|x_n|\,|y_n - M|
+
|M|\,|x_n - L|.
\]

This separates the expression into
\[
(\text{bounded quantity}) \times (\text{small error}).
\]

Because convergent sequences are bounded, there exists $B$ such that
\[
|x_n| \le B.
\]

Hence
\[
|x_n y_n - LM|
\le
B|y_n - M| + |M|\,|x_n - L|.
\]

Both terms can be made arbitrarily small.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{3. The Safety Margin Technique (Reciprocals)}

\paragraph{Logical situation.}

Division introduces a new danger: if the denominator becomes very
small, the fraction becomes very large.

Suppose
\[
x_n \to L
\qquad \text{with } L \neq 0.
\]

We want to control
\[
\left|\frac{1}{x_n} - \frac{1}{L}\right|.
\]

To do this we must ensure that $x_n$ never approaches zero.

\paragraph{Algebraic manipulation.}

Begin with the reverse triangle inequality:
\[
|x_n|
=
|L - (L - x_n)|
\ge
|L| - |L - x_n|.
\]

Because $x_n \to L$, we can ensure
\[
|x_n - L| < \frac{|L|}{2}.
\]

Substituting gives
\[
|x_n|
>
|L| - \frac{|L|}{2}
=
\frac{|L|}{2}.
\]

Thus the denominator remains bounded away from zero.

Taking reciprocals yields
\[
\frac{1}{|x_n|}
<
\frac{2}{|L|}.
\]

We have obtained a controlled upper bound for the reciprocal.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{4. The Add--Subtract Trick (General Strategy)}

\paragraph{Logical situation.}

Often we wish to control a distance
\[
|A - C|
\]
but cannot simplify it directly.

The strategy is to introduce an intermediate quantity $B$ that shares
structure with both $A$ and $C$.

We then measure the journey
\[
A \to B \to C.
\]

The triangle inequality guarantees that the direct distance cannot
exceed the length of this two-step path.

\paragraph{Algebraic template.}

Insert the intermediate value:
\[
|A - C|
=
|A - B + B - C|.
\]

Applying the triangle inequality yields
\[
|A - C|
\le
|A - B| + |B - C|.
\]

This technique allows us to

\begin{itemize}
\item introduce common factors,
\item isolate small errors,
\item transform complicated expressions into manageable pieces.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{Summary}

In analysis the triangle inequality is not merely a geometric
statement.

It is a systematic method for decomposing errors.

Every application follows the same pattern:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Identify the distance that must be controlled.
\item Introduce appropriate intermediate points.
\item Apply the triangle inequality.
\item Bound the resulting pieces.
\end{enumerate}

Mastering these decompositions is one of the most important skills in
real analysis.

\subsection{Triangle Inequality Toolbox}
\label{sec:triangle-inequality-toolbox}

\paragraph{Core Principle.}

Every decomposition ultimately comes from the triangle inequality
\[
|A - C| \le |A - B| + |B - C|.
\]

The guiding strategy in bounding arguments is:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Identify the distance to control.
\item Insert an intermediate point.
\item Apply the triangle inequality.
\item Bound the resulting pieces.
\end{enumerate}

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{1. Sum Decomposition}

Used when studying sums of convergent sequences.

\[
|(x+y) - (L+M)|
=
|(x-L) + (y-M)|.
\]

Applying the triangle inequality,
\[
|(x-L)+(y-M)|
\le
|x-L| + |y-M|.
\]

\textbf{Interpretation.}

The total error of a sum is bounded by the sum of the individual errors.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{2. Difference Decomposition}

Used when controlling differences of quantities.

\[
|(x-y) - (L-M)|
=
|(x-L) - (y-M)|.
\]

Hence
\[
|(x-L)-(y-M)|
\le
|x-L| + |y-M|.
\]

\textbf{Interpretation.}

Subtracting two approximations introduces two independent errors.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{3. Product Decomposition}

Used constantly in limit proofs.

\[
xy - LM
=
x(y-M) + M(x-L).
\]

Thus
\[
|xy - LM|
\le
|x|\,|y-M| + |M|\,|x-L|.
\]

\textbf{Interpretation.}

Change one factor at a time.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{4. Quotient Decomposition}

Used when analyzing reciprocals or ratios.

Begin with the identity
\[
\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{L}
=
\frac{L-x}{xL}.
\]

Taking absolute values,
\[
\left|\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{L}\right|
=
\frac{|x-L|}{|x||L|}.
\]

\textbf{Interpretation.}

Control the numerator using convergence and ensure the denominator stays
bounded away from zero.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{5. Square Decomposition}

Often appears in polynomial limits.

\[
x^2 - L^2 = (x-L)(x+L).
\]

Therefore
\[
|x^2-L^2|
=
|x-L|\,|x+L|.
\]

\textbf{Interpretation.}

Factorization isolates the small quantity $x-L$.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{6. Difference of Squares / Polynomial Trick}

For two moving quantities,

\[
x^2 - y^2 = (x-y)(x+y).
\]

Hence
\[
|x^2-y^2|
=
|x-y|\,|x+y|.
\]

\textbf{Interpretation.}

Factorization converts nonlinear change into linear change.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{7. Iterated Triangle Inequality}

Used for sums of many terms.

\[
|a_1+a_2+\dots+a_n|
\le
|a_1|+|a_2|+\dots+|a_n|.
\]

\textbf{Interpretation.}

The total deviation is bounded by the sum of the individual deviations.

\bigskip

When facing a bounding problem, analysts mentally check the structure
of the expression.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\textbf{Structure of expression} & \textbf{Toolbox rule} \\
\hline
sum & Rule 1 \\
difference & Rule 2 \\
product & Rule 3 \\
quotient & Rule 4 \\
squares & Rule 5 \\
polynomial difference & Rule 6 \\
long sums & Rule 7 \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Recognizing the algebraic structure tells you which decomposition to apply.

squares and polynomial differences are handled by factorization, and long
sums are treated by repeated insertion of intermediate steps.
\end{remark*}

\subsection{The Iterated Triangle Inequality}
\label{sec:iterated-triangle-inequality}

\subsubsection*{1. The Basic Triangle Inequality}

The triangle inequality expresses a simple geometric fact: the direct
distance between two points is never greater than the distance obtained
by traveling through an intermediate point.

\[
|x+y| \le |x| + |y|.
\]

Equivalently,

\[
|A-C| \le |A-B| + |B-C|.
\]

This tells us that one distance can be bounded by two shorter distances.
In analysis, this idea is used constantly to break complicated
expressions into manageable pieces.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{2. Extending the Triangle Inequality}

Often we must control the magnitude of a sum involving many terms:

\[
a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \dots + a_n.
\]

The triangle inequality can be applied repeatedly to obtain

\[
|a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_n|
\le
|a_1| + |a_2| + \dots + |a_n|.
\]

This result is called the \emph{iterated triangle inequality}.  
It is simply the triangle inequality applied multiple times.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{3. Why the Inequality Is True}

To understand the result, consider the case of three terms.

Start with

\[
|a_1 + a_2 + a_3|.
\]

Group the first two terms:

\[
| (a_1 + a_2) + a_3 |.
\]

Applying the triangle inequality gives

\[
| (a_1 + a_2) + a_3 |
\le
|a_1 + a_2| + |a_3|.
\]

Now apply the triangle inequality again:

\[
|a_1 + a_2|
\le
|a_1| + |a_2|.
\]

Combining these results yields

\[
|a_1 + a_2 + a_3|
\le
|a_1| + |a_2| + |a_3|.
\]

Repeating this argument shows that the same inequality holds for any
number of terms.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{4. Geometric Interpretation}

The iterated triangle inequality has a natural geometric meaning.

Imagine walking along a path consisting of several segments.  
The total displacement from the starting point to the ending point is
never greater than the total length of the path.

Thus

\[
\text{direct distance} \le \text{sum of path lengths}.
\]

Each term in the sum represents the length of one step along the path.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{5. Why the Iterated Inequality Is So Useful}

The iterated triangle inequality appears constantly in analysis because
it allows complicated expressions to be bounded term by term.

For example, suppose we want to bound

\[
|(x_n - L) + (y_n - M) + (z_n - N)|.
\]

The iterated triangle inequality gives

\[
|(x_n - L) + (y_n - M) + (z_n - N)|
\le
|x_n - L| + |y_n - M| + |z_n - N|.
\]

Each term can now be controlled separately.

This is precisely the type of decomposition used in many
$\varepsilon$--proofs.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{6. Typical Uses in Analysis}

The iterated triangle inequality appears in many contexts.

\paragraph{Bounding sums of convergent sequences.}

When proving

\[
x_n + y_n + z_n \to L + M + N
\]

the iterated triangle inequality separates the errors.

\paragraph{Controlling series.}

If

\[
S_n = a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_n,
\]

then

\[
|S_n|
\le
|a_1| + |a_2| + \dots + |a_n|.
\]

This inequality is fundamental in the study of series.

\paragraph{Bounding error terms.}

Many estimates involve expressions such as

\[
|e_1 + e_2 + e_3|.
\]

The iterated triangle inequality allows the total error to be bounded by

\[
|e_1| + |e_2| + |e_3|.
\]

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{7. Conceptual Insight}

The iterated triangle inequality reveals an important principle:

\[
\text{Total deviation}
\;\le\;
\text{sum of individual deviations}.
\]

This idea lies at the heart of many arguments in real analysis.

Instead of controlling a complicated quantity directly, we break it into
pieces whose sizes we understand.

\bigskip

\subsubsection*{Key Intuition}

Nearly every bounding argument in analysis follows the same pattern:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Express a complicated quantity as a sum of simpler pieces.
\item Apply the iterated triangle inequality.
\item Bound each piece individually.
\end{enumerate}

Once this strategy becomes familiar, many proofs in analysis become much
easier to construct.

\subsubsection*{A Chain Diagram for the Iterated Triangle Inequality}

The iterated triangle inequality can be visualized by thinking of the
sum
\[
a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n
\]
as a sequence of successive steps on the real line.

Define the partial sums
\[
s_0 := 0,
\qquad
s_1 := a_1,
\qquad
s_2 := a_1+a_2,
\qquad
\dots,
\qquad
s_n := a_1+\cdots+a_n.
\]

Then the quantity
\[
|a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n| = |s_n-s_0|
\]
is the direct distance from the starting point \(s_0=0\) to the ending
point \(s_n\), while
\[
|a_1|+|a_2|+\cdots+|a_n|
=
|s_1-s_0|+|s_2-s_1|+\cdots+|s_n-s_{n-1}|
\]
is the total length of the path obtained by traveling through all the
intermediate partial sums.

Thus the iterated triangle inequality says:
\[
|s_n-s_0|
\le
|s_1-s_0|+|s_2-s_1|+\cdots+|s_n-s_{n-1}|.
\]

steps.
\end{remark*}
