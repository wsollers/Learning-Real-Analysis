\section{Polynomial Basis}

In ordinary calculus, polynomial functions are naturally expressed using
the powers

\[
1, x, x^2, x^3, \dots
\]

because derivatives behave simply on these functions:

\[
\frac{d}{dx} x^n = n x^{n-1}.
\]

In discrete calculus, however, the difference operator does not behave as
cleanly on ordinary powers.  For example,

\[
\Delta (n^2) = (n+1)^2 - n^2 = 2n + 1.
\]

Although the degree decreases, the result contains additional terms.
For this reason it is convenient to introduce a different basis of
polynomials that interacts more naturally with the difference operator.

\subsection*{Falling Factorials}

\begin{definition}[Falling Factorial]
For a nonnegative integer \(k\), the \emph{falling factorial} is defined by

\[
x^{\underline{k}} = x(x-1)(x-2)\cdots(x-k+1).
\]

By convention,

\[
x^{\underline{0}} = 1.
\]
\end{definition}

The first few falling factorials are

\[
\begin{aligned}
x^{\underline{0}} &= 1, \\
x^{\underline{1}} &= x, \\
x^{\underline{2}} &= x(x-1), \\
x^{\underline{3}} &= x(x-1)(x-2).
\end{aligned}
\]

\subsection*{Difference of Falling Factorials}

The falling factorials interact very cleanly with the difference operator.

\begin{theorem}
For \(k \ge 1\),

\[
\Delta x^{\underline{k}} = k\,x^{\underline{k-1}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Observe that

\[
(x+1)^{\underline{k}} =
(x+1)x(x-1)\cdots(x-k+2).
\]

Then

\[
\Delta x^{\underline{k}}
=
(x+1)^{\underline{k}} - x^{\underline{k}}.
\]

Factoring the common terms gives

\[
\Delta x^{\underline{k}}
=
x(x-1)\cdots(x-k+2)\left[(x+1)-(x-k+1)\right].
\]

Simplifying,

\[
(x+1)-(x-k+1)=k.
\]

Thus

\[
\Delta x^{\underline{k}} = k\,x^{\underline{k-1}}.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This formula closely resembles the familiar derivative rule

\[
\frac{d}{dx} x^k = kx^{k-1}.
\]

Because of this simple behavior under the difference operator,
falling factorials form the natural polynomial basis for discrete calculus.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Polynomial Representation}

Every polynomial function can be written as a linear combination of
falling factorials:

\[
f(x) =
a_0
+
a_1 x
+
a_2 x^{\underline{2}}
+
a_3 x^{\underline{3}}
+
\cdots
+
a_n x^{\underline{n}}.
\]

This representation plays the same role in discrete calculus that
ordinary power series play in continuous calculus.

\begin{remark}
Using the falling factorial basis greatly simplifies computations
involving finite differences and leads naturally to the
\emph{Newton series}, the discrete analogue of the Taylor expansion.
\end{remark}
