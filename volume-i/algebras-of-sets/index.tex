\medskip
\noindent\textbf{How we got here.}
The chapter on sets, relations, and functions established the raw
vocabulary: what a set is, how functions transfer information between
sets, and how relations impose structure on a single set.  We worked
with $\cup$, $\cap$, $\setminus$, and complement as individual
operations, but we did not ask what happens when a \emph{collection}
of sets is closed under some or all of them.  That question is
exactly what this chapter answers.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{What this chapter builds.}
We study four families of set collections, each defined by which
closure properties it satisfies: Boolean algebras of sets (finite
operations, complement), rings of sets (finite operations, no
complement required), $\sigma$-algebras (countable operations,
complement), and Borel sets (the $\sigma$-algebra generated by open
sets on $\mathbb{R}$, treated here at the definitional level only).
Every proof is a pure set argument --- element chasing, De Morgan
manipulation, and diagonal constructions --- with no topology or
measure theory assumed.  The Borel section contains honest stubs
marking where metric topology will later plug in.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Where this leads.}
The Boolean algebra of sets is the finite combinatorial backbone that
appears in every subsequent chapter involving collections of sets.
The ring and $\sigma$-algebra machinery feeds directly into Halmos's
\emph{Measure Theory} Chapter~I when the measure theory track begins.
The Borel section is completed in the metric topology chapter once
open sets are available as objects.  The cardinality chapter
following this one supplies the tools needed to prove that
$\mathfrak{B}(\mathbb{R}) \subsetneq \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R})$.
\end{tcolorbox}
\vspace{1em}

\subsection*{Structural Roadmap}
Each major topic is organised as:
\begin{center}
\textbf{Definitions $\longrightarrow$ Closure Properties
$\longrightarrow$ Structural Consequences and Counterexamples}
\end{center}
The global progression is:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Set operations and the hierarchy of closure conditions
        (the shared foundation for all subsequent sections).
  \item Boolean algebra of sets: all fourteen classical laws proved
        from element-chasing primitives; symmetric difference as
        ring addition; the Boolean ring structure of $\mathcal{P}(X)$.
  \item Rings of sets: weakening complement closure; the canonical
        example of half-open intervals; every algebra is a ring but
        not conversely.
  \item $\sigma$-Algebras: passing from finite to countable closure;
        generated $\sigma$-algebras; the countable/co-countable
        $\sigma$-algebra as a concrete working example.
  \item Borel sets: the $\sigma$-algebra generated by open sets on
        $\mathbb{R}$; definitions and the non-surjectivity of
        $\mathfrak{B}(\mathbb{R})$ onto $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R})$
        via cardinality (deferred to cardinality chapter); stubs
        for topology-dependent proofs.
\end{enumerate}
